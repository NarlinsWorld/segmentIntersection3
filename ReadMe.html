Intersection with the Perp Operator

If vector $$u = \binom{x}{y}\tag{Eq 1}$$ then vector $$u^{\perp} = \binom{-y}{x}\tag{Eq2}.$$ It's just a definition.

Given two lines, $$X=P+t\cdot\mathbf{u}\tag{Eq 3}$$ and $$X=Q+s\cdot\mathbf{v},\tag{Eq 4}$$ and given that they are not parallel, find their intersection, $X.$

Answer: First, equate the two equations, then subtract point $Q$ from both sides. $$ P+t\cdot\mathbf{u}=Q+s\cdot\mathbf{v} \tag{Eq 5} $$ $$ (P-Q)+t\cdot\mathbf{u}=s\cdot\mathbf{v} \tag{Eq 6} $$

Now multiply both sides by $\mathbf{u^{\perp}}$. Since $\mathbf{u\cdot u}^{\perp}=0,$ the $t\cdot\mathbf{u}$ term will disappear. $$ (P-Q)\cdot\mathbf{u^{\perp}}=s\cdot\mathbf{v\cdot u}^{\perp}\tag{Eq 7} $$ Since the lines are not parallel, $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}$ is not zero and it is admissible to divide by it. A test for parallel is $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}=0?$ $$ s=\frac{(P-Q)\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}}{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}}\tag{Eq 8} $$

Of course, we cannot cancel out the two vectors, $\mathbf{u^{\perp}},$ since that would lead to an inadmissible vector division, but the dot product, $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}$ is a scalar. Finally, we can substitute $s$ into Eq 4 to get the following intersection. $$ X=Q+\frac{(P-Q)\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}}{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}^{\perp}}\cdot\mathbf{v}.\tag{Eq 9} $$
